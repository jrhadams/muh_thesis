\chapter{Summary}
\label{cha:Summary}

Potato (\textit{Solanum tuberosum L.}) currently stands as the world's third most important food crop after rice and wheat. Potato provides essential nutrition and calories to billions of people globally. Its tubers are prized not only for their versatility in culinary applications but also for their nutritional value, offering a rich source of carbohydrates, vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants. Beyond its role as a staple food, potato serves as a critical raw material for industrial applications including starch production, processed foods, and bio-based materials. Despite its essential role in international food security, genetic improvement of potato faces formidable challenges and is constrained by multiple biological and market-related factors. The crop's tetraploid nature, clonal propagation system, and complex genetic architecture complicate breeding efforts and negatively affect innovation. Furthermore, potato's susceptibility to numerous pests and diseases, coupled with its sensitivity to environmental stresses, necessitates continuous genetic innovation. These realities coupled with long breeding cycles characteristic of clonally propagated crops hamper the creation of novel potato varieties. Hybrid and diploid breeding systems have been voiced as one potential solution which could circumvent some of the issues facing traditional clonal breeding. Hybrid breeding systems are inherently true seed based which allow for faster propagation and multiplication rates in a breeding pipeline. True potato seed can be leveraged in replicated early yield and multi-environmental trialling resulting in higher quality evaluations of important traits such as yield. This then accelerates genetic gain by allowing for selection on complex traits earlier in the breeding cycle. Moreover, following the pattern of other hybrid crops, this trial data can be further utilized in predictive modelling methodology which can further quicken selection decisions in the inbred parent development. The thesis takes the following form.

In \textbf{Chapter 1} I begin with a gentle introduction of the evolutionary and recent cultivated history of potato. This is followed by a justification for genetic improvement in potato and lay out current popular breeding methods for clonal potato. This includes an examination of specific factors of potato's biology which directly inform breeding; and how it is hampered by potato's tetraploidy, obligate clonal propagation, and deleterious mutation load. Here, I focus on hybrid breeding methodology and assess where it can potentially augment genetic gain using diploid potato. However, to properly evaluate whether hybrid breeding can do what it has promised, a proper quantitative evaluation must be made using extant material. This chapter concludes by describing this knowledge gap and the aims of this thesis.

\textbf{Chapter 2} begins by describing the structure  the evaluation of an F1 hybrid multi-environmental trial (MET). This chapter focuses primarily on estimating intra-trial spatial variation and statistically delineating several genetic components of variation, namely additive and non-additive genetic effects operating within this hybrid crossing block. I consider how these genetic fractions for several tuber traits might impact breeding strategies and selection decisions.

\textbf{Chapter 3} examines the potential of multiple METs for training genome-wide regression models. This analysis is conducted on several heritable traits, with specific attention to different genetic parameterizations to assess the prediction accuracy of each model. The genomic models tested are analogous to those examined in \textbf{Chapter 2}, with the addition of a model validation procedure specifically designed for F1 hybrid datasets.

\textbf{Chapter 4} provides a broader evaluation of genomic models and assesses their potential in several selection scenarios. Specifically, the efficiency of genomic selection is contrasted with marker-assisted selection (MAS) schemas based on putative quantitative trait loci (QTL) in a panel of F1 hybrids. The primary aim is to evaluate whether quantitative trait improvement in diploid hybrid potato could be more efficient with MAS-based procedures compared to genomic selection approaches.

\textbf{Chapter 5} addresses the question of molecular marker information and whether multiallelic marker data offers any utility in predictive applications. Two forms of multiallelic information are considered: short-range haplotypes (or haplotags) and founder-origin variants (or identity-by-descent based variants). I examine the utility of both forms of multiallelism through a broad survey of predictive genomic models to assess their benefits over traditional SNP-based predictors.

\textbf{Chapter 6} concludes with a synthesis of the previous experimental chapters, with particular emphasis on how these findings advance biometrical applications in hybrid potato. We conclude by offering a survey of currently under-served research topics and discussing their relevance to hybrid potato breeding systems, providing direction for future investigations in this emerging field.
