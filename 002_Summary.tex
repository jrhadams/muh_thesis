\chapter{Summary}
\label{cha:Summary}

The potato (\textit{Solanum tuberosum L.}) currently stands as the world's third most important food crop after rice and wheat. Potato provides essential nutrition and calories to billions of people globally. Its tubers are prized not only for their versatility in culinary applications but also for their nutritional value, offering a rich source of carbohydrates, vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants. Beyond its role as a staple food, potato serves as a critical raw material for industrial applications including starch production, processed foods, and bio-based materials. Despite its role in international food systems, genetic improvement of potato faces formidable challenges and is constrained by multiple biological and market-related factors. The crop's tetraploid nature, clonal propagation system, and high heterozygosity create complex genetic architectures that complicate traditional breeding approaches. Furthermore, potato's susceptibility to numerous pests and diseases, coupled with its sensitivity to environmental stresses, necessitates continuous genetic innovation. These biological constraints, combined with the lengthy breeding cycles characteristic of clonally propagated crops, have meant that only over the past century have concentrated efforts yielded significant advances in potato genetic improvement.

In response to these challenges, hybrid breeding has emerged as a promising solution that could revolutionize potato improvement. By shifting from clonal propagation to true seed production through diploid hybrid systems, breeders can potentially accelerate genetic gain, enhance genetic diversity, and simplify breeding operations. This thesis explores the biometrical foundations of hybrid potato breeding, examining how modern statistical and genomic approaches can be applied to optimize selection in F1 hybrid populations. The transition to hybrid breeding represents not merely a technical innovation but a paradigm shift that could unlock potato's full genetic potential for future generations.

\textbf{Chapter 2} begins with the evaluation of an F1 hybrid multi-environmental trial (MET). This chapter focuses primarily on estimating intra-trial spatial variation and statistically delineating several genetic components of variation, namely additive and non-additive genetic effects operating within this hybrid crossing block. I consider how these genetic fractions for several tuber traits might impact breeding strategies and selection decisions.

\textbf{Chapter 3} examines the potential of multiple METs for training genome-wide regression models. This analysis is conducted on several heritable traits, with specific attention to different genetic parameterizations to assess the prediction accuracy of each model. The genomic models tested are analogous to those examined in \textbf{Chapter 2}, with the addition of a model validation procedure specifically designed for F1 hybrid datasets.

\textbf{Chapter 4} provides a broader evaluation of genomic models and assesses their potential in several selection scenarios. Specifically, the efficiency of genomic selection is contrasted with marker-assisted selection (MAS) schemas based on putative quantitative trait loci (QTL) in a panel of F1 hybrids. The primary aim is to evaluate whether quantitative trait improvement in diploid hybrid potato could be more efficient with MAS-based procedures compared to genomic selection approaches.

\textbf{Chapter 5} addresses the question of molecular marker information and whether multiallelic marker data offers any utility in predictive applications. Two forms of multiallelic information are considered: short-range haplotypes (or haplotags) and founder-origin variants (or identity-by-descent based variants). I examine the utility of both forms of multiallelism through a broad survey of predictive genomic models to assess their benefits over traditional SNP-based predictors.

\textbf{Chapter 6} concludes with a synthesis of the previous experimental chapters, with particular emphasis on how these findings advance biometrical applications in hybrid potato. We conclude by offering a survey of currently under-served research topics and discussing their relevance to hybrid potato breeding systems, providing direction for future investigations in this emerging field.
